What is the GuzhengAir

As the name suggests, the GuzhengAir is an electronic instrument designed to simulate the playstyle and unique sound of a traditional Chinese guzheng. To play the GuzhengAir, place a hand or finger (typically the thumb) in front of the main ultrasonic sensor. Then, when the finger is lifted, resembling the motion of plucking a string, a sample of a guzheng note is played.

The instrument is tuned to the G major pentatonic scale, range D4-D5; and the pitch of the sample is shifted according to the finger's proximity to the sensor before "plucking"- lower notes are played closer to the sensor, whereas higher notes are played closer to the player.

Why make the GuzhengAir

When figuring out what instrument I wanted to create, I considered many ideas. I was inspired by existing gestural instruments, so I decided to create an instrument with its own unique gesture. I set my mind on a plucking motion and realized I could reimagine the guzheng.

Throughout my life, I have pursued many ways of connecting with my Chinese heritage; part of this was discovering a love of traditional Chinese instruments and music. In my life, I have learned many instruments, of both Western and Eastern origin, and I would have loved to learn the guzheng. But there are a number of reasons why I was less successful with the guzheng than other instruments:

  • Guzheng technique is significantly right-hand dominant; I'm left-handed.
  • The guzheng is not easily portable; they are just over 5 feet in length and weigh between 20 and 30 pounds.
  • Guzhengs, like many instruments, are expensive; they range from a few hundred dollars to a couple thousand, not including strings, picks, etc.
In addition to my desire to create a unique gesture, I aim to solve these issues with my design of the GuzhengAir.

My Process

My first step was to make pitch shifts over a continuous sine wave using the two ultrasonic sensors, not dissimilar to a theremin. The main sensor, which faces the player, is used to determine pitch; the playable pitches were limited to the G major pentatonic scale. The secondary sensor, which faces upward, bends the pitch determined from the main sensor up to 100 cents. This is to mimic the other half of a traditional guzheng, which is used to play bends and vibrato.

After figuring out the pitch shift mechanics, I replaced the sine wave with an audio sample of a guzheng playing D4. I looped the sample for simplicity at the time. In this step, I realized that the overtones of the original note (D4) were also being shifted, and therefore becoming distorted. A simple low-pass filter was effective enough to solve this issue.

The next issue to tackle was the looping. The motivation of this instrument was the unique play gesture, which at this stage of development was not being used. Adding a both a select object and a onebang object to the Max patch made it so the guzheng sample would only play once when my hand moved out of the main ultrasonic sensor's range.

Next Steps

As I continue to work on the GuzhengAir, I have many additions and improvements that I want to implement:

  1. Exchange the guzheng sample for a generated signal that resembles a guzheng. This should make the pitch shifting easier and sound more realistic, especially for the higher pitches played on the current model that often get distorted.
  2. Add capabilities to play trills and glissandos - techniques and sounds that are characteristic of the guzheng.
  3. Add the ability to play multiple pitches at once.
  4. Add octave-switching functionality. (The guzheng typically spans 4 octaves, whereas the GuzhengAir currently covers one)
I will continue to update this page as this project develops.
